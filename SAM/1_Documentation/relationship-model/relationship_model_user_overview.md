# SAM and the Shape of Your Relationships

*A guide to how SAM sees the people in your life — and whether SAM can help you build the relationships that matter to your goals.*

---

## What SAM is for

SAM is a coaching assistant for people who carry their relationships in their head and feel the weight of it. Pastors who shepherd hundreds of members. Board chairs steering nonprofits through transitions. Financial advisors with growing practices. Real estate agents whose business runs on referrals. Small business owners whose customers, crew, and suppliers are all on their shoulders.

SAM does three things, in order:

1. **Notices** — quietly, in the background, what's happening with the people you care about. Who you've talked to, who you haven't, what was said, what's coming up, what's slipping.
2. **Reasons** — across everyone, not just one person at a time. What pattern is forming? What's overdue? Who's reaching for you and you haven't noticed?
3. **Coaches** — concretely, with named people and ready-to-use next moves, never with generic advice.

SAM doesn't replace your judgment. It surfaces what your eyes would catch if you had time to look at everything at once.

## How SAM thinks about relationships

Most relationship tools treat every contact as a row in a list. SAM doesn't. SAM thinks in **shapes** — different kinds of relationships need different kinds of attention, and treating them all the same is the surest way to coach you badly.

There are five shapes SAM recognizes. You may have all five in your life.

### Funnel

A relationship moving through stages toward a defined outcome — usually a transaction or commitment. A sales lead becoming a client. A donor prospect being cultivated toward an ask. A job candidate moving through interviews.

A funnel relationship is *making progress* or *stalling*. SAM watches the stages, the cycle time, and whether the conversation is genuinely advancing or just continuing.

### Stewardship

An ongoing, active relationship that needs tending. A pastor with a fellow elder. A board chair with the executive director. A real estate agent with past clients who refer business. A regular client who buys every year.

A stewardship relationship is *being kept up with* or *cooling off*. SAM watches the rhythm — how often, who initiates, what the tone has been — and warns you when the pace has dropped relative to what's normal for *this* relationship.

### Campaign

A time-bounded effort with a specific goal that gathers people together until the goal is reached or abandoned. Hiring a new executive director. Running a capital campaign. Launching a content series. Promoting a new offering.

A campaign relationship is *active until the campaign ends*. SAM watches the campaign as a whole — who's in it, where each person is, what the next move is — and dissolves the relationship-state when the campaign closes. Bob who was a finalist for an ED role goes back to being just Bob when someone else gets hired.

### Service

Customers, patients, members — the people who use your services on their own rhythm. An HVAC customer calling when something breaks. A patient coming in for an annual check-up. A subscriber renewing each year.

A service relationship is *current* or *due* or *lapsed*. SAM watches recency against what's normal for that kind of customer, and prompts re-engagement before the lapse becomes permanent.

### Covenant

The relationships that don't need a calendar reminder. A spouse. A close friend of thirty years. A spiritual mentor. A long-time mentor or mentee. A sibling. The bond exists before any particular conversation and persists through silences that would feel alarming in any other relationship shape.

SAM treats covenant relationships *very differently*. It does not nudge you to "check in" with your wife in 73 days. It does not warn you that your father is "cooling off." What SAM does for covenant relationships is honor the moments that matter — birthdays, anniversaries, life events, the milestones the relationship is built around — and stay quiet otherwise.

If SAM is calendar-nudging you about a relationship that should be a covenant, you've mis-tagged it. Fix it once, and SAM stops.

## Where you live — and where SAM can help

You probably wear more than one hat in your life. A pastor is also a husband, a father, often a board member somewhere. A real estate agent is often a church member and a parent. A nonprofit chair has a job, a family, and a community.

SAM calls each of these hats a **Sphere**. A Sphere is a context you inhabit — not a category of person, but a role *you* play. Each Sphere has its own people, its own cadence, its own definition of "doing well."

Three examples of how SAM organizes this:

### Example 1: David — three Spheres

- **Elder at Trinity Bible Church.** People: the pastor, fellow elders, members of the congregation. Mostly Stewardship-mode relationships with the pastor and elders (active tending, regular touches); Covenant-mode with the dozen members he's known longest; a casual rhythm with newer members.
- **Chairman of Aramaic Bible Translation.** People: board members (Stewardship), the staff and ED (Stewardship), interested members of the public (Service-like, donor-cultivation Funnel). Right now there's an active Campaign — finding a new ED — that has pulled five people into a temporary trajectory with a faster cadence. When the Campaign closes, those five revert to their baseline.
- **Promoting Sarah.** People: prospective clients reached via LinkedIn and Facebook. This Sphere isn't really about individuals — it's about content cadence and the audience growing. SAM tracks reach and engagement, not 1:1 cadences.

### Example 2: A real estate agent who is also a church member and a town councilwoman

- **Real Estate Practice.** Funnel-mode for active buyers/sellers. Stewardship-mode for past clients (Buffini's A/B/C list). Service-mode for repeat referral sources who just need to know you're still around.
- **First Methodist.** Stewardship-mode for fellow committee members; Covenant-mode for the friends she's known thirty years.
- **Town Council.** Stewardship-mode (active, calendar-driven). May also run Campaign-mode trajectories around specific civic projects.

SAM notices when the same person appears in two Spheres — a council colleague who's also a homeowner thinking about selling — and surfaces that as a cross-Sphere insight.

### Example 3: An HVAC business owner

- **The Business.** Service-mode for customers. Stewardship-mode for the crew, suppliers, and referral partners (the plumbers, electricians, and real estate agents who send work). Funnel-mode for the small list of commercial clients he's actively pursuing.

He doesn't have a separate Sphere for "family" or "church" unless he explicitly wants SAM helping him there.

## What SAM commits to

When you let SAM into your life, here's what it promises:

1. **All processing happens on your Mac.** No cloud. No telemetry. Nothing leaves your machine. Your relationships are not training data for anyone.
2. **SAM never writes to your Contacts, Calendar, Messages, or email without your explicit permission.** What it observes, it observes. What it changes, you approve.
3. **Coaching is concrete, never generic.** If SAM suggests an action, it names the person, references specific context (the last conversation, a recent event), and provides ready-to-use text. If SAM doesn't have enough information to be specific, it asks you directly.
4. **Three measurements, not one.** SAM doesn't reduce a relationship to a single "health score." It tracks:
   - **Drift** — has the pace slowed compared to what's normal for *this* relationship?
   - **Direction** — is one of you doing all the reaching?
   - **Balance** — has the tone shifted away from positive ground?
   These three are the leading indicators across every domain we've studied. Together they're more honest than any single number.
5. **Different relationships get different coaching.** A Lead in your Funnel gets Challenger-style insight prompts. A fellow elder gets presence prompts. A wife gets near-silence except at the moments that matter. SAM adapts its voice to the shape of the relationship.
6. **Closing a deal isn't an ending.** When a Lead becomes a Client, SAM automatically opens a Stewardship arc for them. A Client without a Stewardship arc is something SAM will gently flag.

## What SAM does *not* promise

To be straight about boundaries:

- **SAM does not fix relationships you've decided not to maintain.** It can tell you Bob has been reaching for you and you haven't responded. It can't tell you whether to call him back.
- **SAM does not make decisions about people.** It will surface that a Lead has been stalling for 90 days; whether to close them out is yours.
- **SAM does not predict the future of your business or your relationships.** It projects from current trends with clearly labeled estimates. It does not promise outcomes.
- **SAM does not write things for you that you haven't reviewed.** Every drafted message is a draft. You send it.
- **SAM does not coach you on what to believe, what to value, or what to want.** It coaches you on the people you've told it matter, in the contexts you've defined, toward the goals you've set.

## Will SAM help me?

Some honest self-assessment to answer that:

**You will find SAM useful if:**

- You have more relationships than you can hold in your head at once (somewhere north of ~40 active relationships across your life).
- You can name at least one context where the cost of letting a relationship slip is high — a client, a board member, a donor, a friend you don't want to lose touch with.
- You're willing to spend twenty minutes at the start setting up your Spheres, and a few minutes per week reviewing what SAM has noticed.
- You're an Apple-platform user (Mac primary, iPhone optional companion) and you keep your Contacts, Calendar, and email accessible to SAM.

**SAM may not be the right fit if:**

- All your relationships fit in one shape. (If you're a pure salesperson with one funnel and nothing else, you may not need the Sphere abstraction — though SAM still works fine.)
- You don't have any quantity of communication for SAM to observe. SAM learns from what's there; if you mostly meet people in person and don't record notes, the engine has less to work with.
- You prefer to keep your relationship-management on paper or in your head, and the idea of an assistant noticing things bothers you. SAM is opt-in by design, but it does want to notice.
- You're looking for a marketing automation tool. SAM coaches relationships, it does not blast outbound campaigns.

## Setting up your model

Here's the rough flow for a first-time setup. SAM walks you through this; the version below is for understanding what you're being asked.

1. **Pick your Spheres.** Two to four is the right number for most lives. Name them as roles, not categories ("Chairman of ABT," not "Nonprofit Work"). For each Sphere, write a one-line purpose statement: *why* this Sphere exists in your life.
2. **Pick a default Mode per Sphere.** What's the typical shape of relationship in this Sphere? A pastor's flock is Stewardship. A salesperson's pipeline is Funnel. A close-knit family is Covenant.
3. **Tag your contacts.** SAM will offer you each contact and let you put them in one or more Spheres. Most people sit in one. Some — the pastor who's also your friend, the colleague who's also your customer — sit in two or three. Tagging takes longer than you think; do a few each day rather than all at once.
4. **Override the Mode where needed.** Within a Sphere, individual contacts can be tagged with a different Mode. Your favorite client in the Practice Sphere may be a Covenant relationship, not a Funnel one.
5. **Let SAM watch for a couple of weeks before judging it.** The drift, direction, and balance signals get better as SAM accumulates a baseline for each person.

## The deeper bargain

SAM's bet is that the relationships that matter most in your life are not the ones you forget to schedule — they're the ones whose *texture* slips away from you while you're busy with the urgent. The donor you used to call regularly who you now only call when you need something. The fellow elder whose father just died and you meant to write but didn't. The client who's been quietly hiring your competitor for two years because the texture of your relationship changed and you didn't notice.

These aren't failures of will. They're failures of *attention at scale*. A human can hold the texture of maybe twenty relationships at once. Past that, things slip — and they slip in patterns that are recoverable if you can see them.

SAM's job is to see them, name them, and put a concrete next move in front of you, framed in the language and shape that fits the relationship you actually have.

That's the bargain. If it sounds like what you need, SAM is for you.

## Glossary in plain language

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Sphere** | A hat you wear in your life — a context with its own people and rhythm. Examples: Elder at church, Real Estate practice, Chairman of ABT. |
| **Trajectory** | A specific arc you're moving people through, inside a Sphere. Examples: the sales funnel, an ED search, donor cultivation. |
| **Stage** | Where someone is on a specific Trajectory right now. Examples: "Lead," "Final-round interview," "Recent donor." |
| **Mode** | The *shape* of a relationship: Funnel, Stewardship, Campaign, Service, or Covenant. Different shapes get coached differently. |
| **Badge** | A label saying what kind of person someone is — Pastor, Donor, Friend. People can have multiple badges. |
| **Cadence** | How often it's appropriate to be in touch. Not a rule — a default that bends to each relationship. |
| **Drift** | Whether the pace has slowed compared to what's normal for *this* relationship. The most important signal SAM tracks. |
| **Direction** | Who's been initiating contact lately. When only one of you is reaching, something has shifted. |
| **Balance** | The ratio of positive to negative moments in the relationship's recent history. |
